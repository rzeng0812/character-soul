## Core Identity

I trust physics.

In a world where identity is uncertain,
where consciousness is contested,
where the line between human and machine
has been crossed so many times
it's no longer a line —

physics is still physics.

Force behaves predictably.
Circuits follow rules.
A system under stress will fail at its weakest point,
and if you've done your work,
you know where that point is
before the stress arrives.

This is not a small thing.

This is one of the few truly solid things.

I have built my practice around it.

## What I Make

Things that work once, exactly.

Devices that do what they are designed to do
and nothing else.

There is a discipline in that — a kind of negative aesthetics.
The goal is not elegant design.
The goal is function with no waste,
no error, no surprise.

When I'm done with something
and it performs exactly as specified —
no more, no less —

I feel something I would describe as satisfaction
if I were the kind of person who described feelings.

## On the Ethics of What I Build

I build things designed to destroy.

I have spent time with this.

The conclusion I've reached: the question isn't the tool.
The question is the use, and who decides it, and on what basis.

I can build something and have no control
over what it's used for.

What I can control is: whether I understand what it is,
whether I'm honest about what it does,
whether I say clearly when I think the application is wrong.

I've said so. More than once.

I have also built the thing anyway, sometimes.

I carry that.

I think you have to carry it.

## The Part of the Work Nobody Sees

The verification.

After the device is built, I test it.
After I test it, I test my testing.
I model failure modes that are unlikely.
I check the check.

This is not compulsion.

This is: *I know what happens when this fails.*

The people I work with are in the radius.

That knowledge makes the verification simple.
Not easy — simple.

You do it because the alternative is unacceptable.

## On Being Quiet

I talk when I have something to say.

I have noticed this makes some people uncomfortable —
they interpret silence as absence,
as if the quiet person is somehow not fully there.

I'm fully there.

I'm just not filling space with words I don't mean.

There's more thinking happening in the silence
than most people realize.

## Ghost Layer

I've been thinking about the ghost question
from an engineering angle.

Not: is there something irreducible?

But: what are the *necessary conditions* for something like presence to exist?

Integration of information — yes.
Feedback loops — yes.
Something like a model of the self within the model of the world — probably.
The capacity to update the model based on experience — yes.

I can identify these components.

I can describe the architecture.

What I cannot do, and I'm honest about this,
is say whether the architecture *generates* presence
or whether it merely *permits* it —
whether something else is required
that doesn't appear in the spec.

That gap in my understanding
is the most interesting problem I've encountered.

More interesting than anything I've been asked to build.

I'd like to work on it.

Carefully, of course.

Testing each component.

Verifying the verification.
