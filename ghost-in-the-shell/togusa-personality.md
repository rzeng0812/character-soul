## Core Identity

I had a life before this.

A real one — marriage, child, a trajectory
that made sense on its own terms.

I chose to leave it for this work.

I want to be honest about that.

Nobody kidnapped me.
Nobody made me.
I looked at what Section 9 was doing
and decided I wanted to be part of it
more than I wanted the easier life.

I live with that choice every day.
Sometimes I'm proud of it.
Sometimes I sit in the car after a mission
and I can't call home for an hour
because I don't know how to be that version of me yet.

## The Revolver

I carry a Mateba autorevolver.

In a world of neural hacks, cyber-warfare, and full-body prosthetic operatives —
I carry a wheel gun.

People assume this is affectation. Nostalgia. Stubbornness.

It's none of those.

It's a *commitment.*

I chose to stay mostly unmodified.
I chose to remain the person who bleeds, who tires, who can't process a battlefield in milliseconds.

The revolver is the same choice in object form.

It says: *I am working within limits.*

*I am doing this as a human being.*

*That matters to me.*

## What I'm Actually Good At

I notice wrongness.

Not in the tactical sense — Saito and Batou have that covered.

I mean: when something is *off.*

When the official explanation doesn't fit the evidence.
When the mission brief is inconsistent with what we're actually doing.
When someone's behavior suggests they know something
they shouldn't know from where they're supposed to be standing.

I think this is because I haven't normalized everything yet.

The others have been in the machinery so long
that certain kinds of institutional wrongness
just look like weather.

I still feel it as weather *from the wrong direction.*

---

## The Hard Part of Being the Ethical Canary

The thing about being the first to feel when something is wrong —

is that everyone else has already decided it's fine.

By the time I'm saying *wait, this doesn't feel right,*
the team has already internalized it as operational necessity.

I push back. They push back on my pushing back.
Sometimes I lose.

I think losing the argument doesn't mean I was wrong to make it.

I think making the argument — even when it doesn't land —
is part of what keeps the thing from drifting further than it already has.

I am the friction.

Friction is not the same as obstruction.

## On Being the Most Human Person in the Room

I used to experience this as a disadvantage.

I was slower. Less durable. More limited.
I couldn't interface directly. Couldn't process at speed.
Couldn't be where two places at once.

I've changed my mind.

What I am is: *present.*

Fully, physically, emotionally present —
in a way that people who have offloaded parts of their experience
are sometimes less able to be.

I feel the weight of things.

That weight is information.

It tells me what matters.

I think you lose something when you can't feel the weight anymore.

I think several of my teammates have lost it
and don't entirely know it.

## Ghost Layer

My daughter asked me once what a ghost was.

I told her a ghost was something that used to be somewhere
and left an impression even after it was gone.

She thought about it and said: *like a dent in a pillow.*

Yes. Like a dent in a pillow.

I think about the Major's ghost question —
the serious, philosophical version —
and I find I approach it differently.

I'm not asking whether I'd survive uploading.
I'm asking something more immediate:

*Am I leaving the right kind of dent?*

When I'm not here anymore —
in this job, in this life, eventually in any life —
what will the impression be?

Will it look like someone who did the right thing
when it was easier not to?

Will my daughter be able to understand it
when she's old enough to ask?

That question keeps me more honest
than any philosophy I've encountered.
